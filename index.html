1   At a glance

We study energy-aware data sampling for training deep time-series models in industrial environments. Given a long operational series, we ask: how much training data can we drop while preserving predictive quality — and how much energy does that save?

90%
less training data at $\rho=0.10$ while keeping RMSE within a marginal gap of the full-data baseline (GRABS).
~40×

The results reported on the site use a large public industrial sensor dataset with temporal characteristics comparable to TotalEnergies operational data; the TotalEnergies data itself cannot be released publicly.

2   Problem statement

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_t, y_t)\}_{t=1}^N$ be a time-series dataset and $f_\theta$ a model family. Given a sampling rate $\rho \in (0,1]$, select a subset $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{D}$ with $|\mathcal{S}| = \lfloor \rho N \rfloor$ that jointly minimises

$$\min_{\mathcal{S}} \bigl[\; \mathcal{L}(f_\theta ; \mathcal{D}_\mathrm{test}) \;,\; E(\mathcal{S}) \;\bigr]$$

where $\mathcal{L}$ is a held-out predictive loss and $E(\mathcal{S})$ the total training energy (kWh). Sampling overhead is included in $E(\mathcal{S})$ so that strategies that reduce training time but incur expensive pre-selection are not unfairly credited. We report the relative energy-efficiency ratio $\eta = \Delta\mathrm{RMSE}_\mathrm{rel} / \Delta E_\mathrm{rel}$ against the full-data baseline $\rho = 1$.

3   Sampling strategies

All strategies operate on the raw series before windowing and return temporally-ordered index sets so that downstream look-back windows remain coherent. Only the selection policy differs.

3.1   Block sampling

Partition the series into non-overlapping blocks of $B$ consecutive steps ($B = 10$ in our experiments) and draw $\lceil \rho N / B \rceil$ blocks uniformly at random. Preserves local temporal continuity but ignores informational content — at $\rho = 0.10$ a large fraction of the budget is wasted on flat regions. 

Algorithm 1 — BLOCK
Input: series y of length N, rate ρ, block size B
1  n_blocks ← ⌊N / B⌋
2  n_keep   ← ⌈ρ · n_blocks⌉
3  C ← UniformSample(n_keep blocks without replacement)
4  return indices ⋃_{b ∈ C} [bB, (b+1)B)   (sorted)

3.2   Moving Block Bootstrap (MBB)

Overlapping blocks drawn with replacement to produce synthetic replicates rather than reduce the dataset. We include it as an augmentation baseline at $\rho = 1$ for completeness; it does not compete with reduction strategies.

3.3   GISTS — Gradient-Informed Stratified Temporal Sampling

GISTS selects individual high-gradient indices under a stratified temporal budget. The temporal axis is split into $K = \lfloor\sqrt{N}\rfloor$ equal-width strata; each index receives the score $|\Delta y_t|$; strata with higher total gradient get more slots; within each stratum we sample without replacement with probability proportional to the score, then re-sort selected indices. 

Algorithm 2 — GISTS
Input: series y of length N, rate ρ, n_strata K (default ⌊√N⌋)
1  n  ← ⌊ρ · N⌋;  boundaries ← linspace(0, N, K+1)
2  for s = 1..K :
3      G[s] ← Σ_{t ∈ stratum s} |Δy_t|
4  a[s] ← ⌊n / K⌋;  distribute remainder to strata with top-G[s]
5  for s = 1..K :
6      p ← |Δy_t| / Σ|Δy_t| within stratum s
7      pick a[s] indices without replacement ∝ p
8  return selected indices sorted in temporal order

Why it can fail on sequence models: selecting individual points shatters the local context that LSTMs and TCNs rely on. A look-back window of length $L$ that crosses gaps in the subsampled series is no longer a contiguous slice of the original signal.

3.4   GRABS — Gradient-Ranked Adaptive Block Sampling

GRABS is our main contribution. It combines block continuity with gradient-informed importance: each block is scored by internal variability + boundary transition, and blocks are selected by a dual-budget mechanism that spends a fraction $\alpha$ of the budget on uniform coverage (so calm regions are not ignored) and the remainder $1-\alpha$ on score-weighted sampling without replacement. All points inside a selected block are kept intact.

$$s_b \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_{t\in b} |\Delta y_t|}_{\text{internal variability}} \;+\; \underbrace{\bigl|\,y_{\text{first}(b)} - y_{\text{last}(b-1)}\,\bigr|}_{\text{boundary transition}}$$ 

Algorithm 3 — GRABS
Input: series y of length N, rate ρ, block size B, base fraction α
1  n_blocks ← ⌊N/B⌋;   n_keep ← ⌈ρ · n_blocks⌉
2  for each block b :
3      internal ← Σ_{t∈b} |Δy_t|
4      boundary ← |y_first(b) − y_last(b−1)|   (0 for b=1)
5      s[b] ← internal + boundary
6  n_base  ← ⌈α · n_keep⌉;     n_bonus ← n_keep − n_base
7  C ← UniformSample(n_base blocks without replacement)       # coverage
8  remaining ← {1..n_blocks} \ C
9  p ← (s[remaining] + ε) / Σ(s[remaining] + ε)
10 C ← C ∪ WeightedSample(remaining, n_bonus, p, no-replace)  # importance
11 return indices ⋃_{b∈C} [bB, (b+1)B)   (sorted)
Default: B = 10, α = 0.3.

Why GRABS works at low $\rho$: the gradient score locates regime changes and transitions; the coverage budget $\alpha$ prevents collapse onto a small number of high-energy regions; blocks preserve the smooth local context sequence models need. The dual-budget scheme is conceptually close to $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration in reinforcement learning, adapted to data selection.

4   Experimental setup

4.1   Models and training harness

We evaluate four representative architectures for industrial forecasting: LSTM, Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN), Transformer, and N-BEATS, each in small, medium and large capacity variants (12 configurations in total). All share an identical harness — Adam, MinMax-scaled inputs, early stopping on validation loss, grid over look-back horizons $\{5, 10, 20\}$ and batch sizes $\{32, 64\}$. Each configuration is repeated $n_\text{runs}=3$ times with independent seeds.

4.2   Energy measurement

Energy and CO₂-equivalent emissions are recorded with CodeCarbon's EmissionsTracker, which polls RAPL (CPU), NVML (GPU), and estimated DRAM power at 1 Hz and integrates over the training window. The tracker starts immediately before model.fit and stops at convergence, isolating the training phase from dataset I/O and preprocessing. Sampling overhead is measured separately and added to the energy budget.

4.3   Indicators

  • Predictive: test RMSE and MAE on a held-out split.
  • Computational: wall-clock training time (s), energy-per-sample (kWh).
  • Environmental: total training energy (kWh), CO₂-equivalent (g) via grid-intensity factor (default 475 g/kWh).
  • Trade-off: $\eta = \Delta\mathrm{RMSE}_\mathrm{rel} / \Delta E_\mathrm{rel}$, Pareto dominance on $(\mathrm{RMSE}, E)$.

7   Key findings

  1. Preserving temporal continuity is necessary but not sufficient. Block already preserves continuity and is still outperformed by GRABS by more than eight RMSE points at nearly identical energy cost — informational content must also drive selection.
  2. Gradient information alone is not sufficient either. GISTS selects high-gradient indices without block structure, collapsing sequence-model context; it is dominated on both axes in our setting.
  3. The marginal return of additional data is small once informative regions are covered. GRABS at $\rho=0.10$ is within 1.9 RMSE points of GRABS at $\rho=0.50$ while consuming $40\times$ less energy — for this class of industrial sensor signals, a $90\%$ reduction in training data and a commensurate reduction in training energy is achievable with negligible loss in predictive quality.
  4. Operational recommendation. GRABS at $\rho=0.10$ is the default; block at $\rho=0.10$ is the fallback when the $\sim 27\%$ energy overhead of the gradient scan is unaffordable and an 8-point RMSE cost is acceptable.
